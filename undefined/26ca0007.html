Course Description

This course presents the topic of data mining from a statistical perspective, with attention directed towards both applied and theoretical considerations. An emphasis will be placed on supervised learning methods.

Topics include: linear and logistic regression, discriminant analysis, shrinkage methods, subset selection, dimension reduction techniques, classification and regression trees, ensemble methods, neural networks, deep feedforward networks, and random forests. Model selection and estimation of generalization error will be emphasized. Considerations and issues that arise with high-dimensional (N<<p) applications will be highlighted. This course includes “online computational labs”, which demonstrate methods and concepts in R, using real data.

Textbooks + Computing

  • The Elements of Statistical Learning: Data Mining, Inference, and Prediction. T. Hastie, R. Tibshirani, and J. Friedman. Second Edition (2011), Springer.

  • An Introduction to Statistical Learning: James, G., Witten, D., Hastie, T., & Tibshirani, R. (2013).

  • Statistical Computing Software: R (http://www.r-project.org/), which can be downloaded at no charge.

Online course structure

This course will proceed in a weekly fashion with asynchronous lectures and online learning. Students will be required to regularly access UB Learns, which will contain weekly course content. Weekly content includes, announcements, homework, video lectures and labs (occasional), and power point slides.

Netiquette: For many people, participating in courses with online components is something new. You’ve probably taken many traditional college courses in the past, and are used to the structure, and cultural norms associated with them. Just like traditional courses, online courses have their own set of rules, which need to be followed.

  • The rules of the classroom are the same regardless of location. Remember just because you’re interacting online, doesn’t mean you stop having respect for your professors, and fellow classmates. You’re communicating with a real person, not a computer screen.
  • Remember your audience. When communicating online it’s important to remember whom you’re communicating with. When sending a message to a professor, please refrain from using “text speak”. For example, 4 Shakespeare never intended for you to type “2B or not 2B”. Also, stay away from typing in all capital letters; it will appear as if you are shouting.
  • Avoid strong language. Language can easily be misinterpreted in an online setting. Be sure to review your work before submitting, making sure the reader won’t be able to misinterpret it as strong, or offensive. Sarcasm does not translate well online. Your audience can’t see your facial expressions. Try to be as straight forward as possible.
  • Read everything, twice. Be sure to thoroughly read all course materials before beginning work on assignments. If you have a question, or need clarification, re-read the materials. You may have glanced over an important detail the first time. It you still have trouble, email the professor.
  • Review all materials before submitting. When responding to discussion board posts, be sure to real all-previous postings before you post your own. This way you will not duplicate comments. Also, it is a good idea to write and save your work in a text file first, to prevent information loss due to technical issues.

Policy Regarding Absences, Attendance, Assignments, and Incompletes

  • Class Attendance and Absences: Class attendance is expected in the sense that you are required to keep up with the weekly lectures.
  • Late Assignments: All assignments are due in the digital dropbox (UB Learns) at the designated time and due date. Alternatively, they may be turned in at class, or put in the instructor’s department mailbox (709 Kimball Tower). Failure to submit an assignment on the due date will result in a loss of 5 points per day that the assignment is late. Assignments that are more than three days late will not be accepted. If there are circumstances that will preclude you from turning in assignments on the due date, it is imperative that you discuss the situation with the instructor prior to class.
  • Incomplete Grades for the Course: Incomplete grades will be given only if there are extenuating circumstances that preclude the student from completing the course. The student must have satisfactorily performed in the course up until the time it is requested. Resolution of an incomplete grade will be carried out according to University Policy.
